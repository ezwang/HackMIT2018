Opioid Dependency and Addiction Resources

Medication-Assisted Treatment

Medication-Assisted Treatment for opioid dependency has been shown to decrease opioid use and deaths from overdose as well as improve social functioning and treatment adherence. Medication-Assisted Treatment undertaken during pregnancy also reduces the occurance of neonatal abstinence syndrome in newborns. Though Medication-Assisted Treatment is effective and beneficial for a lot of people, less than half of individuals seeking treatment for opioid dependence receive medication. Consider whether you would like to talk to a physician about seeking Medication-Assisted Treatment.

Naltrexone

Naltrexone can be prescribed by any healthcare professional with the ability to prescribe medications. It can be taken daily in pill form, or monthly as an injection. Naltrexone works by blocking opioid receptors, decreasing the euphoric and sedative effects of opioids. Individuals must completely stop using opioids for 7-10 days before starting on naltrexone.

Methadone

Methadone is typically dispensed at licensed clinics. It can be taken in pill, liquid, or wafer form, and is taken daily. Methadone is an opioid, so administration reduces cravings and withdrawal symptoms for other opiates.

Buprenorphine

Buprenorphine can be prescribed or dispensed by qualified physicians outside of a clinic setting. Buprenophine medication often also includes naloxone to reduce the potential for misuse. It comes in films or tablets which dissolve on the inside of the cheek or under the tongue. Buprenophine acts by activating opioid receptors, but to a lesser extent than many commonly abused opioids. In this way, it reduces or eliminates withdrawal symptoms. Individuals must abstain from using opioids for 24-48 hours before starting buprenophine treatment.
